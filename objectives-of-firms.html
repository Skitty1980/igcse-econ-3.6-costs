What is an Objective?

Definition

An OBJECTIVE is a clearly defined goal or target that a firm wants to achieve.

Objective 1
Survival
Staying in business when times are tough — the most basic goal of any firm.
Objective 2
Social Welfare
Prioritising benefits to society and people beyond just making money.
Objective 3
Profit Maximisation
Operating where TR − TC is at its highest — the classic commercial goal.
Objective 4
Growth
Increasing the size of the firm, its markets, and its operations over time.

Objective 1 — Survival

Definition

SURVIVAL — actions taken by firms to remain operational or in existence.

Most relevant when:
Starting a new business
Facing recession
Fighting tough competition
Vietnamese Context

When COVID-19 hit Vietnam in 2020, many restaurants and shops focused on survival — keeping the doors open until customers returned. Revenue dropped but fixed costs (rent, staff) still had to be paid.

Key Insight

In the long run, firms must be PROFITABLE to survive. Survival is a short-term, crisis-mode objective — not a permanent strategy.

Scenario
A new banh mi shop opens next to 5 established competitors on the same street in Ho Chi Minh City. What is the new shop's primary objective likely to be? Why?

SURVIVAL.

As a new firm, they need to:

Objective 2 — Social Welfare

Definition

SOCIAL WELFARE — economic activity that benefits society beyond just the firm itself, prioritising the wellbeing of others.

1
Enhances brand image

Customers prefer to buy from firms they see as responsible and ethical.

2
Attracts & retains employees

Workers want to feel proud of where they work — CSR improves morale and reduces staff turnover.

3
Appeals to ethical investors

ESG investors increasingly choose socially responsible firms for their portfolios.

Public Sector

Public sector firms (run by government) often prioritise social welfare over profit — e.g. public hospitals, state schools. Their goal is to serve citizens, not shareholders.

Supporting farmers' livelihoods by paying fair prices is a social welfare activity — it benefits others beyond the firm.
A fashion brand uses child labour in overseas factories to keep production costs low.
No — Not Social Welfare
Exploiting children directly harms their welfare and violates their rights — this is the opposite of a social welfare objective.

Objective 3 — Profit Maximisation

"The one responsibility of business towards society is the maximisation of profits to shareholders within the legal framework and the ethical custom of the country."

— Milton Friedman (1976 Nobel Prize in Economics)
Profit = TR − TC
Total Revenue minus Total Costs
Definition

PROFIT MAXIMISATION — operating at the output level where the difference between total revenue and total costs is greatest.

1
Reward for owners (ROI)

Shareholders invest money and expect financial returns — profit is the reward.

2
Finance for growth & R&D

Retained profit is the cheapest internal source of finance for expansion.

3
Long-run survival

Without long-term profit, firms face bankruptcy and must eventually close.

Common Error — Do Not Confuse These!

⚠️ REVENUE is money coming in from sales. PROFIT is what is LEFT after paying ALL costs. A firm can have high revenue but still make a loss.

Profit Max — Quick Calculation

Scenario
A pho restaurant earns $10,000 in revenue this month. Its total costs are $7,000. Calculate its profit.
▶ Click to reveal step-by-step working
Step 1: Identify the formula → Profit = TR − TC
Step 2: Substitute the numbers → Profit = $10,000 − $7,000
Profit = $3,000
Follow-up: Is the firm achieving profit maximisation?
▶ Click to reveal the answer
We cannot tell yet!

The firm is making a profit, but profit maximisation means producing the QUANTITY where profit is highest. The restaurant might earn even more profit by:
• Selling more bowls of pho (if costs rise slower than revenue)
• Adjusting prices to attract more customers

Simply making profit is not the same as maximising it.
Challenge: The next month revenue rises to $12,000 but costs rise to $9,500. Has profit increased?
▶ Click to reveal
New profit = $12,000 − $9,500 = $2,500
Previous profit = $3,000

No — profit has FALLEN by $500 even though revenue increased! This shows why firms track profit, not just revenue.

Objective 4 — Growth

Definition

GROWTH — an increase in the size of a firm and its operations.

1
Higher sales revenue

More customers and more products sold means more money coming in.

2
Higher profits

A larger customer base generates more profit for shareholders and reinvestment.

3
Economies of scale

Larger firms spread fixed costs over more units — lower cost per unit produced.

4
Reduced risk

Operating in multiple markets means one bad market doesn't destroy the firm.

Internal Growth
Own Resources

The firm grows using its own profits and resources — e.g. opening new stores using retained profit. Slower but keeps control.

External Growth
Mergers & Partnerships

The firm grows by combining with others — e.g. Volkswagen Group owns Audi, Bugatti, Lamborghini, Porsche, Bentley, and Ducati. Faster but more complex.

Key Point

Firms often pursue MULTIPLE objectives at once. The priority changes over time — a new firm focuses on survival, a mature firm may focus on growth or profit maximisation.

b) State the formula for profit.
▶ Click to reveal

(2) Reduce costs — bulk buying coffee beans, more efficient store operations, automating some tasks — lowers TC.

Quick Quiz: True or False?

Click each statement to reveal TRUE or FALSE with explanation

1. Survival is only relevant for new firms — established firms never need to worry about survival.
False Even well-established firms may need to focus on survival during recessions or industry shocks.
2. Firms must be profitable in the long run to survive.
True Without profit, a firm eventually runs out of money and must close.
3. Social welfare objectives focus on maximising profit and growth.
False Social welfare prioritises benefits to society, not profit — those are different objectives.
4. Public sector firms typically charge higher prices to ensure profitability.
False Public sector firms usually charge LOWER prices (or are free) to make services affordable for all citizens.
5. Firms that act unethically risk damaging their corporate image and reputation.
True Unethical behaviour leads to lost customers, bad press, and long-term harm to the brand.
6. Profit is the same as revenue.
False Profit = Revenue MINUS Costs. Revenue is money coming in; profit is what's left after all costs are paid.
7. Profit maximisation is the top priority for traditional commercial businesses.
True For-profit companies generally prioritise maximising profit for shareholders as their primary objective.
8. Internal growth occurs when a firm grows by merging with other firms.
False That is external growth. Internal growth uses the firm's own resources (e.g. retained profits to open new stores).
9. External growth can be achieved through mergers with other firms.
True A merger combines two firms into one — this is a key form of external growth.
10. Market share measures a firm's sales as a proportion of the whole industry's total sales.
True Market share = (firm's sales ÷ industry's total sales) × 100%. It shows a firm's competitive position.
