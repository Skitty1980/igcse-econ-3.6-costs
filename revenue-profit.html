Revenue & Profit

Grade 9 — VinSchool Grand Park

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:
  • Define Total Revenue (TR) and Average Revenue (AR)
  • Calculate TR and AR from data
  • Understand how revenue, costs and profit connect
  • Note: Marginal revenue is NOT required at IGCSE

What is Revenue?

Definition

Revenue is the MONEY COMING IN to a business from selling goods and services

Common Mistake — Don't Confuse These!

Revenue is the total money received from sales. Profit is what's left after paying all costs. A business can have huge revenue but still make a loss if costs are high.

Type 1
Total Revenue (TR)
All money received from all sales
Type 2
Average Revenue (AR)
Revenue per unit sold

Total Revenue (TR)

TR = P × Q
P = Price per unit  |  Q = Quantity sold
Definition

The total money a firm earns from selling its products

  • Revenue goes UP when price rises (if same quantity sold)
  • Revenue goes UP when more units are sold (at same price)
Worked Example

A banh mi shop sells 100 banh mi at $3 each

TR = $3 × 100 = $300

The shop earns $300 in total revenue from those sales.

Average Revenue (AR)

AR = TR ÷ Q
Revenue per unit sold
Key Insight
AR = Price (P)

They are mathematically the same thing. Why?

TR = P × Q   so   AR = TR ÷ Q = (P × Q) ÷ Q = P

If you know the price, you know AR — they are the same number.

Definition

Average Revenue is the revenue received per unit sold. At IGCSE, AR always equals the price charged.

What is Profit?

Profit = TR − TC
Total Revenue minus Total Costs
Definition

Profit is what is LEFT OVER after the firm has paid all its costs

TR > TC
PROFIT
Revenue exceeds costs
TR < TC
LOSS
Costs exceed revenue
TR = TC
BREAK-EVEN
Revenue covers costs exactly

Revenue vs Profit

Revenue
Money IN

All the money coming in from sales — before any costs are deducted

Profit
Money LEFT

What remains after all costs have been paid — could be positive or negative

Worked Example
TR
$1,000
100 items × $10
TC
$750
Total costs
Profit
$250
$1,000 − $750
Big Takeaway

Revenue is NOT the same as profit! Always subtract total costs from total revenue to find profit.

Exam Practice 1

Given Information

A firm has monthly fixed costs of $3,000 and unit variable costs of $2. Its current level of demand is 3,000 units each month. The average unit price is $6.

Part (a) [2 marks]: Calculate the firm's current average costs each month.
▶ Click to reveal step-by-step working
Step 1: TVC = $2 × 3,000 = $6,000
Step 2: TC = TFC + TVC = $3,000 + $6,000 = $9,000
Step 3: ATC = TC ÷ Q = $9,000 ÷ 3,000 = $3
Part (b) [2 marks]: Calculate the profit if demand increases to 3,500 units per month.
▶ Click to reveal step-by-step working
Step 1: New TVC = $2 × 3,500 = $7,000
Step 2: New TC = $3,000 + $7,000 = $10,000
Step 3: TR = P × Q = $6 × 3,500 = $21,000
Step 4: Profit = TR − TC = $21,000 − $10,000 = $11,000

Exam Practice 3

Given Information

A firm has fixed costs of $500 and sells 200 units per month. Each unit sells for $35 and has average variable costs of $15.

Part (a) [2 marks]: Calculate the total costs per month.
▶ Click to reveal
Step 1: TVC = AVC × Q = $15 × 200 = $3,000
Step 2: TC = TFC + TVC = $500 + $3,000 = $3,500
Part (b) [2 marks]: Calculate the firm's profit for the month.
▶ Click to reveal
Step 1: TR = P × Q = $35 × 200 = $7,000
Step 2: Profit = TR − TC = $7,000 − $3,500 = $3,500
Part (c) [2 marks]: Calculate the change in average costs at 100 units vs 200 units.
▶ Click to reveal
At 100 units: TC = $500 + ($15 × 100) = $2,000 → ATC = $2,000 ÷ 100 = $20
At 200 units: TC = $500 + ($15 × 200) = $3,500 → ATC = $3,500 ÷ 200 = $17.50
Change = $20 − $17.50 = $2.50 decrease
Part (d) [2 marks]: Comment on why the unit cost has dropped.
▶ Click to reveal
The fixed costs of $500 are spread over more units of output.
AFC at 100 units = $500 ÷ 100 = $5.00
AFC at 200 units = $500 ÷ 200 = $2.50
This is an example of economies of scale — producing more units lowers the cost per unit.

Summary — Revenue & Profit

Key Formulas
  • TR = P × Q
  • AR = TR ÷ Q = P
  • Profit = TR − TC
  • Loss if TR < TC
  • Break-even if TR = TC
Key Definitions
  • TR — Total money from all sales (P × Q)
  • AR — Revenue per unit = Price
  • Profit — TR minus TC
  • Revenue — money coming in (not profit!)
  • Break-even — TR exactly equals TC
Three Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • 1. Don't confuse COST (firm pays) with PRICE (customer pays)
  • 2. Don't confuse REVENUE (money coming in) with PROFIT (money left after costs)
  • 3. Remember AR = P — they are the same thing mathematically
The Big Idea

Revenue is the starting point. Subtract all costs and you get profit. A business can have millions in revenue but still make a loss if its costs are higher.
