What are Costs?

  • Every firm must spend money to produce goods or services — these are its costs of production
  • There are two main types of costs: Fixed Costs and Variable Costs
  • Together they form Total Costs — everything the firm spends

Fixed Costs (FC)

Definition

Costs that do NOT change with the level of output

  • Must be paid even if the firm produces nothing
  • They exist whether output is 0 or 10,000 units
Examples:
Rent Insurance Manager salaries Machinery leasing Business rates
Common Mistake

Fixed costs are NOT costs that never change. Rent CAN increase — but it doesn't increase because you produced more.

Fixed costs don't change with OUTPUT — that's the key idea.

Think of it this way:

A Vietnamese restaurant pays $3,000/month rent. Whether they serve 10 customers or 500 customers that month — the rent bill is still $3,000.

Variable Costs (VC)

Definition

Costs that change directly with the level of output

  • The more you produce, the higher your variable costs
  • VC = $0 when output = 0
  • Key difference from FC: variable costs only exist when producing
Examples:
Raw materials Production worker wages Electricity for machines Packaging Delivery fuel
Think of it this way:

A banh mi shop uses flour, meat, and vegetables in every roll. Make 100 rolls → buy 100 sets of ingredients. Make 0 rolls → buy nothing.

At zero output: VC = $0
As output rises: VC rises with it

Total Costs (TC)

TC = TFC + TVC
Total Costs = Total Fixed Costs + Total Variable Costs
  • Total cost is everything the firm spends to produce its output
  • At zero output: TC = FC (because VC = $0 when nothing is produced)
  • As output rises: TC rises because VC rises — FC stays the same
  • TC curve is parallel to TVC — shifted upward by the amount of TFC

Why does TC start at TFC?
When a firm produces zero output, it still has to pay its fixed costs (rent, insurance, etc.). Variable costs are $0 because nothing is being made. So TC = TFC + $0 = TFC. That's why the TC line begins at the TFC line, not at the origin.
What does the gap between TC and TFC mean?
The vertical distance between the TC line and the TFC line at any output level equals the total variable cost (TVC). Since TC = TFC + TVC, the gap is always TVC. As output rises, TVC rises, so the gap gets bigger.

Average Total Cost (ATC)

ATC = TC ÷ Q
Cost per unit of output
ATC = AFC + AVC
Average Fixed Cost + Average Variable Cost
  • Tells us how much each unit costs to make
Why does ATC fall then rise?
At low output:
ATC is high — fixed costs are spread over very few units. Each unit carries a big chunk of FC.
As output rises:
ATC falls — fixed costs are spread over more units. This is economies of scale.
At very high output:
ATC may rise — firm becomes too big, inefficient. Diseconomies of scale.

Average Fixed Cost (AFC)

AFC = TFC ÷ Q
Fixed cost per unit of output
  • AFC ALWAYS falls as output increases
  • Same fixed cost is spread over more units
  • This is the core logic behind economies of scale (from 3.4)
Worked Example — TFC = $200
Output (Q) TFC ($) AFC ($) = TFC ÷ Q
50 200 4.00
100 200 2.00
200 200 1.00
400 200 0.50
AFC fell from $4.00 to $0.50 — the fixed cost is "spread" over 8× more units. This is why producing more reduces cost per unit.

Step 2: ATC = TC ÷ Output
Step 3: AFC = TFC ÷ Output
Step 4: AVC = TVC ÷ Output
Notice the pattern: AFC fell from $4.00 to $0.50 as fixed costs spread over more units. AVC fell then rose (from $3.00 to $2.50 to $3.00) — variable cost efficiency improves then worsens. ATC fell from $7.00 to $3.50 — the savings from spreading FC offset rising AVC. This is the beginning of the U-shaped ATC curve!

Average Variable Cost (AVC)

AVC = TVC ÷ Q
Variable cost per unit of output
  • AVC measures how much variable cost each unit adds
  • At low output: AVC may be high (small-scale production inefficiency)
  • As output grows: AVC may fall (specialisation, efficiency gains)
  • At very high output: AVC may rise again (overcrowding, bottlenecks)
Putting it all together:
ATC = AFC + AVC
Total average cost = fixed cost per unit + variable cost per unit
AFC always falls as Q rises
Because the same TFC is spread over more and more units
⚠️ Exam tip: What happens to averages when output = 0?
You cannot calculate ATC, AFC, or AVC when output is zero — dividing by zero is impossible. At zero output, simply state: TC = TFC (the only cost is fixed costs).

Worked Example (1)

A firm produces four units of output at an average total cost (ATC) of $18. The firm subsequently makes a fifth unit which costs the firm $20.

What is the value of the average total cost of the five units of output?

Step-by-step solution
Step 1: Find Total Cost of the first 4 units — TC = ATC × Q = $18 × 4 = $72
Step 2: Add the cost of the 5th unit — $72 + $20 = $92 (new TC)
Step 3: Calculate new ATC — ATC = TC ÷ Q = $92 ÷ 5 = $18.40
✅ Answer: ATC = $18.40 per unit

Common Mistake — Economies of Scale

The #1 mix-up students make in exam questions

The wrong definition students often give:
"Economies of scale are costs of production that fall as a firm increases its production."

This is incorrect. As a firm increases the scale of its production, it will actually cost MORE to produce additional output — because the firm needs more raw materials, more component parts, more labour, and more fuel.

However, it is possible that the average cost of production falls — NOT the overall production cost (total cost).

The correct definition:

Economies of scale occur when the average cost per unit (ATC) falls as output increases. Total costs still rise with output — but each unit becomes cheaper to produce because fixed costs are spread over more units and the firm gains efficiency.

Exam tip: Always say "average cost per unit falls" — never "costs fall" on its own.

Summary — Firms' Costs

Key Definitions
  • FC — Costs that do NOT change with output
  • VC — Costs that change directly with output
  • TC — Total of all costs (FC + VC)
  • ATC — Average cost per unit (TC ÷ Q)
  • AFC — Fixed cost per unit (TFC ÷ Q)
  • AVC — Variable cost per unit (TVC ÷ Q)
Key Formulas
  • TC = TFC + TVC
  • ATC = TC ÷ Q
  • ATC = AFC + AVC
  • AFC = TFC ÷ Q
  • AVC = TVC ÷ Q
  • At Q = 0: TC = TFC, VC = $0
What happens as output rises?
  • TFC stays constant (horizontal line)
  • TVC rises from zero (smooth curve)
  • TC rises (parallel to TVC, shifted up by TFC)
  • AFC always falls (spreading fixed costs)
  • ATC falls then may rise (U-shape)
Exam Tips
  • Fixed costs can change over time — just NOT with output
  • VC = $0 at zero output — always state this
  • TC starts at TFC intercept, not at the origin
  • AFC falls because same FC is shared over more units
  • Link ATC to economies of scale (Topic 3.4)
The Big Idea

Firms want to find the output level where Average Total Cost (ATC) is lowest — this is where they are most efficient and profitable.
